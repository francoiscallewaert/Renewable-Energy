CONCLUSIONS

  • 35GW wind and solar can be installed with high marginal value (low waste)
  • Beyond, more pumped hydro storage needed
  • Fossil fuels could be decreased from 9 to 2% and nuclear from 72 to 59%
  • Investment opportunity = $40B for wind and $25B for solar
  • Data analytics: battery control (Stem), solar panel inspection (ABJDrone), wind maintenance prediction (GE Predix)

Model implementation

  • This model considers the hourly power production by source during three years (2015-2017).
  • Because nuclear power fluctuates very slowly at the scale of the week, and corresponds to more than 2/3 of the total production, we subtract it from the total to focus on the highly variable part of the production.
  • "Power profile" page allows to change the desired amount of wind, solar and storage. The model predicts the behavior of the grid during 3 years given those inputs. Here is a short summary of how the calculations are done:

    • The main challenge here is to balance sources that cannot be controlled (wind, solar) with sources that can be controlled (hydroelectricity, nuclear and fossil fuels) such that production = consumption at any time. If production > consumption, energy is wasted, which is uneconomical. If production < consumption, there is a blackout (shortage), which must be avoided absolutely. Additionally, the model is designed to reduce the amount of fossil fuels to a negligible amount (1-2%).
    Hydro and storage have similar characteristics, especially given that storage is based on pumped hydro technology. For hydroelectricity, we assume lakes have a full capacity of 3600GWh, a refill rate of 1.5GW and an output between 0 and 8.5GW. Pumped hydro does not produce anything, but it can store some energy, then release it later with an efficiency of 85%. The maximum in/out power is 4GW for a total storage of 180GWh (current situation), and we assume it grows linearly when we model larger amount of storage, up to +/-13GW for 1800GWh storage.
    The model can be described as a control system, with (total consumption - historic nuclear production) as target output. Then, wind, solar and nuclear are automatically added to production, as nuclear cannot be controlled in short term. If production is too high, excess production is stored into pumped hydro. If the production is too low, a combination of hydro and storage are used to match consumption. If this is not enough or if hydro/storage are too low, fossil fuels are used to match the short-term shortage, and nuclear output is slowly increased to ensure a stable long-term situation and decrease fossil fuels use.
